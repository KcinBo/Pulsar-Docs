# The Treasury

The treasury is the economic core of Pulsar Protocol. It holds assets owned or controlled by the protocol and may deploy them under an approved investment and risk policy.

## What the treasury may hold

Treasury reporting should separate assets into clear categories:

* liquid stable reserves;
* Onchain assets such as WPLS and PLSX;
* Ethereum and approved multichain assets;
* protocol-owned liquidity positions;
* lending, staking, or liquidity strategies;
* directional investments;
* strategic or venture positions, if authorized;
* cash equivalents, hedges, and operational reserves.

Gross treasury value is not the same as immediately available cash. Some assets may be volatile, illiquid, bridged, locked, encumbered, or exposed to another protocol.

## Productive capital

Pulsar's thesis is that part of the treasury can be put to productive use instead of remaining entirely idle. Potential returns may come from trading fees, lending, staking, liquidity provision, incentives, or realized investment gains.

Productive strategies introduce additional risk. Before capital is deployed, policy should define approved venues, position limits, custody, leverage restrictions, counterparty limits, exit conditions, reporting, and emergency authority.

## Backing measurements

Pulsar should report more than one treasury number:

* **Gross treasury value:** market value of all counted assets before liabilities.
* **Net treasury value:** counted assets minus liabilities and committed obligations.
* **Liquid treasury value:** assets expected to be readily accessible with limited price impact.
* **Risk-adjusted treasury value:** asset values after published haircuts.
* **Protocol-owned liquidity:** treasury-owned liquidity positions, reported separately to reduce circular valuation.

Treasury backing is an accounting measure, not an automatic redemption promise. Unless a contract provides a specific redemption right, holders cannot assume they can exchange PULSAR for a proportional share of treasury assets.

## Transparency standard

The final dashboard should identify assets, networks, contract addresses, valuation sources, material liabilities, realized performance, unrealized performance, strategy exposures, and the time of the latest update.
