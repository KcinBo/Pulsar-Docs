# Market dynamics

Pulsar's systems interact with the open market. Understanding the feedback loops is more useful than assuming they always move in one direction.

## During expansion

A higher PULSAR market premium may create room for attractive mint terms. Successful mints can bring new assets into the treasury. A larger treasury may expand liquidity, productive positions, and market confidence. More staking may reduce immediately liquid supply. Together, these forces can strengthen demand and create additional mint capacity.

This is a reflexive loop: confidence and capital inflows can reinforce one another.

## During contraction

The loop can reverse. A falling token price can remove mint discounts, slow treasury inflows, reduce confidence in emissions, and encourage unstaking or selling. Treasury assets may fall at the same time as PULSAR, especially when positions are concentrated in correlated crypto assets. Thin liquidity can accelerate the move.

Treasury earnings can reduce reliance on market premium, but they do not eliminate market, strategy, liquidity, or governance risk.

## Different participant goals

- **Stakers** generally care about long-term share, rewards, and purchasing power.
- **Minters** generally care about the quoted terms and PULSAR's price during vesting.
- **Liquidity providers** care about fees, incentives, volatility, and impermanent loss.
- **The protocol** should care about net risk-adjusted treasury value, liquidity durability, liabilities, and sustainable earning power.

Good policy tries to align these interests without pretending they are identical.

## Price, backing, and premium

PULSAR may trade above or below any published backing measure. A premium can reflect expected future growth, utility, liquidity, or speculation. A discount can reflect market stress, doubts about valuation, illiquidity, expected dilution, or a lack of redemption rights.

Backing is information. It is not a hard price floor.
